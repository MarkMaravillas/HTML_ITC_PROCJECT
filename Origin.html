Origin of Chess
Chess originated in India around the 6th century as a game called Chaturanga, which represented the four branches of the military: infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots. It spread to Persia, where it evolved into Shatranj, introducing key terms like "Shah" king and "checkmate." From there, the game moved into the Islamic world, where scholars further developed its rules and strategy, making it a symbol of intellectual pursuit. By the 9th to 11th centuries, chess reached Europe, where it underwent significant changes, including the introduction of the modern queen and bishop. Chess was standardized in the 19th century, leading to the first World Chess Championship in 1886, and has since become a global intellectual and competitive pursuit.


Before Chess it was Chaturanga
Chaturanga, originating in ancient India around the 6th century where the game was played on an 8x8 board, and its pieces represented the military divisions of the time these are infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots. It was not just a game but a strategic tool that reflected the importance of military tactics and battlefield formations. Played by nobility and scholars, Chaturanga was an intellectual pursuit that mirrored the ancient Indian society's focus on warfare, wisdom, and strategic thought. The game gradually spread across regions, evolving through Persia into the game of Shatranj, and then into the chess we recognize today
Modern Chess
Modern chess is a highly intellectual, competitive game that has evolved into an internationally recognized sport. Played on an 8x8 grid, chess is a game of strategy and foresight where two players control an army of 16 pieces each, with the ultimate goal of checkmating the opponent's king. Today's chess tournaments are broadcast globally, with players using a blend of deep calculation, pattern recognition, and strategic planning. The game has adapted with technology, with online platforms offering real-time matches and analysis, making it more accessible and inclusive. Chess now serves as a symbol of intellectual prowess, attracting a global audience from casual enthusiasts to elite grandmasters.
